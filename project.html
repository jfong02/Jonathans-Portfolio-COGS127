User Research and Problem Statement

Empathizing and Needfinding

Our primary objective was to understand the user's perspective to better improve quality and/or ease of communication while using the Lyft service. Our target user was the rider side of the Lyft service, due to a higher population of users and a greater magnitude of user needs.

There were 3 primary research goals which we hoped to answer:

Understand what drives communication among users and drivers of ride-sharing applications

Find breakdowns in the communication process or trends that made communication between riders and drivers unpleasant

Isolate areas of the application that could lead to more streamlined communication between rider and driver

Research Methods and Findings

We utilized a mixture of survey and interviews, which helped us gain a general overview of rider needs while diving deep to obtain more anecdotal response that may uncover pain points and breakdowns. Responses and interviews were obtained from riders as well as drivers, as drivers have a vast level of exposure to several riders and may be able to identify certain trends among their riders.

After collecting feedback from user research, we discovered several findings that helped guide our eventual problem statement. One major finding was that people do not like being forced into a conversation or feel like they must talk with their driver. Rather, organic conversation is much more preferred. Furthermore, although there was a sizeable chance that conversation could lead to a negative ride experience, conversation was much more likely to turn a ridesharing experience into a positive experience. Based on our user research, we came up with the following problem statement.

Problem Statement

Lyft riders need a way to better notify their drivers of their preferences and expectations regarding conversation, comfort, accessibility and service throughout the ride-sharing experience. There must be non-intrusive application tweaks that will significantly increase the quality of communication, trust, and overall ride satisfaction between both parties and increase brand equity.

Formulating Possible Solutions

Once we had a problem statement and mindset for the project, we began formulating solutions based off of potential stakeholders and their needs.

Personas

We began looking at several potential personas who would make up our target user group. Each persona had a different motive and personality, and was based on some of the trends found from the user research performed prior to this stage.

Jenny - The Social Butterfly

Extroverted, Great Talker, Friendly, Funny
Biography
A recent graduate from Ohio State, Jenny is taking some time traveling to different cities to explore new environments and meet new people. Jenny actively seeks out human connection through conversation. She is curious about the human condition, social experience, and thrive in social settings. Social silence makes her feel awkward so she feels that vacuum with her own voice when it presents itself.
Needs
  • While she is traveling, she often takes Lyft to get to places like airports and hotels.
  • She would like to have nice conversations with the drivers about the cities they are in and explore different cultures.
Pain Points
  • Some drivers do not talk much during the ride so she would think the ride experiences are silent and awkward.

Elon - Mister Industrious

Hard Worker, Indifferent, Pefectionist, Industrious
Biography
A project lead for a big tech firm, Elon is always busy with his work. He works hard and strives to earn a good living. He likes to be organized himself, and tends to fix little things in his life into his model of perfection. Yet he does not care much about others and is often nonchalant. He takes Lyft to his work every morning, and usually he is on his computer or tablet preparing for his conference or presentation during the whole ride.
Needs
  • Elon takes Lyft everyday to work.
  • During the ride, he expects a quiet and undisturbed experience while he takes care of work-related issues.
Pain Points
  • Sometimes the drivers keep talking to him while he prepares for his work in the car, and he feels disturbed and annoyed.
  • He does not like when a driver drives too slow or take an alternative route so it would make him late for work.
  • As an organized and clean person, he does not like when the car smells bad.

Kiro - The Foreigner

Ambivert, Friendly, Studious, Gentle
Biography
As an international student from Tokyo, Kiro is used to the public transportation from home. Now he is studying at UCLA and does not have a car, he has to use Lyft to get to places during the weekends, and even sometimes to campus. He usually does not initiate conversation in public scenarios partly due to the fact that he is still learning English, but still can have a great time chatting with other people so that he can learn things about his new environment and practice the language. Kiro also does not have the best constitution and could get sick easily if he does not dress properly or the temperature is too high or too low.
Needs
  • Kiro takes Lyft to campus and other places in a city whose public transportation system is not so advanced.
  • Kiro is not a native English speaker so he would prefer drivers to speak in an easy-to-follow manner with concise wording.
  • Kiro would like the temperature in the cars to be his preferred setting.
Pain Points
  • When the driver speaks too fast so he could not understand, he gets frustrated.
  • When the AC in the car is set to a temperature too cold, or when the windows are open all the way during the ride, Kiro may get headache afterwards.

Malina - The Silent One

Introverted, Reserved, Shy, Anti-Social
Malina is quiet with everyone around her. Maybe only two or three close friends of her could make her talk for over a few minutes straight. She does not enjoy most of the social convention. Public speaking is a nightmare to her, and talking to strangers is definitely out of the picture. She only stays in her comfort zone. Now, as a third year college student, she moved out of campus and has to take Lyft to school since she does not drive.
Needs
  • She does not drive herself so she takes Lyft to campus when she has classes.
  • She expects no conversation at all with the drivers.
  • She would like to feel comfortable and safe during the ride.
Pain Points
  • She hates when drivers start talking to her, and ask her about her personal information.
  • She gets car sick easily, so if a driver drives the car in an erratic way she would not feel so good afterwards.

UX Flow

With our primary research complete, we set out to formulate potential solutions. After collaborating together as a team, we discussed potential approaches, and eventually narrowed down our solutions to four Entry Points in the UX flow we wanted to redesign, which was created in Figma. These included language and accessibility, conversation and clothing options, mid-drive options, and a contextual feedback system.

Entry Point 1

Our primary goal here was to add accessibility information to better communicate with the driver. We also felt that adding a language area would help to communicate to the driver the rider's preferred means of communication and any difficulty the rider may have communicating to the driver.

Entry Point 3

For our more sensitive users, we created an in-ride panel of options to adjust some of their desired car environments. This could also apply to those who did not desire conversation, yet would still be able to communicate to their driver in specific ways.

Entry Point 2

Some responses from our user research indicated that different levels of conversation may result in a better ride experience. We developed this feature so that the rider would be able to make the decision of how much they would be willing to talk, which would improve ride quality. Additionally, we also included a clothing option tab and a special request box so that the rider could better communicate to their driver who they were when being picked up, as well as any special requests they might have. All these features woud ideally be optional for the rushed user.

Entry Point 4

The catalyst for this area of redesign was based on our user research, where we asked users to describe their most positive or negative experiences using a rideshare application. We realized that this information would be extremely useful to potential riders, but the 5-star rating alone could not sufficiently communicate this information. To resolve this issue, we wanted to include a contextual rating system that would better communicate both to drivers and riders a more holistic ride experience, whether it be a positive or negative experience.

Low-Fidelity Prototyping

Informed Design Iterations & User Feedback

With an initial design on paper, we decided to shift gears and return back to perform an initial round of user testing to make more informed design decisions. We developed two different prototypes each for our most high profile entry points, and proceeded to perform A/B testing to determine the right direction to take our redesign.

Entry Point 1

In Entry Point 1, our goal was to make design choices that would be low-impact and seamlessly connect with the current profile page UI of Lyft. We added additional accessibility impairment toggles and a new language section, where you could select your preferred language to communicate in, as well as a scale for the user's level of comfort in English.

Entry Point 2

In Entry Point 2, we developed 2 different versions of this redesign. One was a full-length scrollable page which would contain all the additional information the user could input under collapsible tabs. The other was based on a pop-up functionality, which would have overlayed windows to display the options for each new feature. The 3 main features added were a conversation level setting, a clothing setting to communicate to the driver what the user would be wearing for easy pickup, and a special request box.

Entry Point 3

Entry Point 3 was completely built with communication issues of the user in mind. We wanted to provide a way for the user to communicate with the driver if verbal communication was, for some reason, interrupted. This could be on the user side if they had a hearing impairment or had trouble verbally communicating with the driver, or if the user didn’t want to converse with the driver, but still had information they would like to communicate with the driver. These options would be available to the user during the ride based on their needs: add a stop, roll up/down the windows, change the A/C, adjust the music volume, or modify the level of conversation between the user and the driver.

Entry Point 4

Entry Point 4 was also the second area of our redesign with 2 versions to compare between. In this entry point, we utilized a label-based rating system to replace the current 5-star rating, and included both positive and negative labels. We chose to use label-based choices to make this rating system faster and easier to fill out. Our A/B versions differed by essentially making version A combine all parts of the post-trip menu into one full-height screen, whereas version B would have a 2 part menu, where one would be ratings and the second payment details.

User Testing & Feedback

Using the paper prototype, 3 randomly chosen participants were given four tasks to perform:

Change accessibility preference. (Entry Point 1)

Set clothing color, conversation preference, and special request. (Entry Point 2)

Set mid-drive options. (Entry Point 3)

Rate the driver after the ride has ended. (Entry Point 4)

Point of View

With one person acting as the "computer" to manipulate the paper screens while performing A/B testing, we were able to identify the main takeaways for our redesign.

Overall, our redesign was met with generally positive responses, with only slight confusion shown in a couple areas. Our A/B testing yielded interesting insights that we may utilize to improve the UI of each of the entry points. In all our designs, the main trend was that the design must be inclusive, require little cognitive effort to understand and use, and clear and direct.

High-Fidelity Prototyping

Finalizing the Concept

Once we had a solid idea of the main components of the UI, we took our low-fidelity prototypes and converted them to high-fidelity prototypes with Figma. We continued developing multiple versions to continually iterate and improve on the design, until we ended with with our final prototype. One of the main areas of focus here was to develop the redesigned UI with the style of Lyft in mind. Many of the decisions and finishing touches made here impacted the finalized product.

Final Redesign

We decided to obtain additional feedback from our peers with another two versions of the same screen, which was invaluable to our final design.

Overall, there were mixed feedback from both version A and B, with our users liking version B's options of choosing the clothing type but preferred version A's speed and ease of use. We took the feedback obtained and combined together the best from both versions to produce the final result.To reduce usage errors, we decided to reduce the number of colors and increase the size of the color selection palette, while combining the functionality of the clothing selector in a quick and easy selection window.

The finalized review screen is an updated version of the alternative version (Type B). The feedback we received relayed a tendency to like the single-screen version better. We took the non-Lyft-like UI style and revamped it to match the Lyft theme, which was a vast improvement. In addition, we noticed that some of our users may be in a rush and not want to rate their driver with such a detailed review scheme, so we included a skip to payment button to finish the ride as fast as possible, with the ability for the user to review the ride later. Finally, we added an option to email or text a summary of the trip with payment details.

The Polished Design

The Final Proposed Design and its Features

At the end of our quarter-long journey, we present our design with its many facets of improvements. Below you will find the 4 major components of our redesign:

Language and Accessibility

Three new options are added in the setting - “Vision Impaired”, “Hearing Impaired”, and Preferred Language. The users can choose the first two options using a toggle button. For preferred language, if the users click on it, they will be brought to another page, where the user can choose their preferred language and choose their level of English fluency so that the drivers would have a better idea about their customer’s background, promoting better understanding while in vehicle.

Conversation, Clothing, and Other Requests

Three new buttons are added to the page when the driver is en route to pick up the passenger. A clothing option for the users to select their current color of clothes, a chat preference option for them to select set conversation expectations, and an other request option which includes a textbox for any additional information they want to tell the driver before getting in the car. The clothing selector allows the rider to better tell the driver who they are and improve pick-up efficiency. The chat preference selector is intended to reduce awkwardness or disturbances during the ride by informing the driver of the rider's conversation expectations. The request box allows the user to add additional information that may be helpful for the driver, so that the driver is prepared immediately upon pickup. All three options are not required for the users to do, and are designed to be inputted during the free time the user has while waiting for the driver to arrive.

Mid-Drive Notifications

During the ride, if the users are not willing or able to talk to the drivers for any reason, the users can use this screen to adjust their preference in the car including the chat preference, window level, fan control, and music volume. This would then send a notification to the driver, who can then adjust the settings to the users' preferences

Contextual Rating

Our final product emerged with a single full-length scrollable page to eliminate having to click through multiple submit buttons. Rating labels would be categorized between positives and negatives, and an option to add user-generated labels is included. From previous user feedback, we included the option for users to skip the rating if in a rush and come back at a later time to finish the review.

Reflection

Food for Thought

Throughout the course of the design process, I have been able to sharpen my design skills even more. I again had a fresh realization of the importance of user research in design, particularly when tackling a real-world problem with many implications and nuances. Without a proper grounding in the understanding of user needs, it is impossible to appropriately design for that specific user, and this class has taught me this valuable lesson. Now looking back, I realize that despite the many good ideas this redesign brings, this redesign could have been approached with a different lens. I found that during the whole process, I was constantly trying to ideate and figure out ways of redesigning several pain points throughout the app, instead of than focusing on a single pain point and making sure it sticks the landing. Although the features developed here may have been with good intent, looking back on it, I am not certain whether people would be willing to use our features.

The final redesign created throughout this project should not be considered an ending point. Rather, it ought to be only the first step to further redesign and development. In further redesigns of this proposed solution, I would consider designing more closely aligned towards more singular solutions, rather than this project's more expansive reach into the Lyft UX flow. I realized it is easier to define a single feature rather than a group of features, and addressing every deficient area in the app simultaneously may spread the design team too thinly to appropriately address each problem space. Although this final redesign is a good step towards the right direction in improving communication among ridesharing services, more detailed redesign may be needed to fully flesh out each part of the app. In the future, I fully intend to take the lessons learned from this app and not design for the sake of design, but rather design with the intent of designing something that the users would be more willing and even happy to use.
